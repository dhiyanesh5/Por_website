1. Context — The Problem
IIT Guwahati had no Kabaddi Club.
I decided to be the one who fixed that.

A campus of 7,000+ students. A sport loved across India. Players practising in isolation — no structure, no budget, no institutional recognition. An obvious gap no one was doing anything about.

The problem was layered: no club meant no budget, no equipment, no entry into Spirit (the flagship inter-IIT sports meet). No precedent meant every system had to be invented from scratch — trials, sponsorships, tournament formats, the lot.

I didn't wait for someone to solve it.

3. Key Decisions
Three calls that
defined everything.
01
Win first. Apply for the club second.
Rather than spending months navigating bureaucracy, I recruited informally, entered Spirit 2024, and won gold. Walking into the Sports Board with a gold medal is a fundamentally different conversation than walking in with a proposal.
Why it was hard — If we lost on debut, there was no club. I was betting everything on our first-ever performance.
02
Apply as Secretary in my most demanding year — with zero senior guidance.
Even after winning, the Sports Board hesitated. I saw the hesitation, knew the momentum would die if I didn't push, and applied to be Founding Secretary going into 4th year. Internships, coursework, projects — all competing. I treated the club like a second job and invented every system from scratch.
Why it was hard — No playbook, no seniors who'd done this before. Every decision made alone under maximum pressure.
03
Fight for women's inclusion when no one else was advocating for it.
When Spirit 25 organizers planned a men's-only event, I intervened. Used contacts from my Open Tournament network, did a second outreach when teams dropped at the last minute, and ensured 3 women's teams competed in the first-ever women's kabaddi event at Spirit. Our own women's team debuted too.
Why it was hard — I had no formal authority over Spirit. I made it happen anyway through relationships and persistence.
4. Execution
From idea
to institution.
Six phases. One year. Zero seniors who'd done this before.
Phase 1 · Pre-April 2025
The Cold Start
No club, no roster, no budget. Recruited players by walking up to people on campus. Entered Spirit 2024 as an informal team and won gold — the first time IITG had ever won at kabaddi. Also started running the club Instagram (@iitg_kabaddi) solo from this point — making posts, editing reels, designing in Figma, adding stories — entirely self-driven, before any official PoR.
🥇 Spirit '24 Gold · Instagram started: ~70 followers
Phase 2 · April 2025
Institutionalisation
Became Founding Secretary. Ran KPL — auction-based intra-institute league, 55 registered players, 70+ participants. 19 sign-ups on registration night one. Secured in-kind sponsorships (trophies + medals). Girls' team formed — first in club history. Dean of Student Affairs attended the finals.
70+ participants · Sponsorship secured · Women's team born
Phase 3 · September 2025
Scaling Up — The Open Tournament
Organised IITG's first-ever inter-college kabaddi championship. 14 teams including state-level squads from Kolkata and Guwahati State senior & junior teams. Secured Multi-Purpose Hall, arranged professional mats from Cotton University, obtained AAKA-certified referees. Live-streamed with Hindi commentary. Promo reel collaboration drove major digital engagement.
14 teams · State-level · Live-streamed
Phase 4 · October 2025
Competitive Dominance
Entered BKC-4 strategically as a Spirit 25 training ground. Finished undefeated. Won gold. Ran women's trials and skills workshop simultaneously — building the first competitive women's squad. Received Best Secretary award at Sports Board mid-tenure review.
🥇 BKC Gold · Undefeated · Best Secretary
Phase 5 · November 2025
Spirit '25 — The Historic Stand
Men's team reached the final against a professional South Kolkata state club — and secured Silver. Women's team competed in the first-ever women's kabaddi event at Spirit, marking a landmark for the club and the institute.
🥈 Men's Silver · ♀️ Women's historic debut at Spirit

Spardha 2026 finals drew 150–175 spectators (DoSA + Chairperson attended). Instagram grew from ~70 to 275 followers. 30-page tenure report documented for successors.

Part 2 · Why I Fit
I don't wait
for a playbook.
I write one.

The problems I enjoy solving are the ones no one has mapped yet. Give me an ambiguous brief and I'll work backwards from the outcome, find the smallest set of moves that unlock momentum, and go. I don't need continuous push to deliver.

That's exactly what I did with kabaddi. The club didn't exist. The Sports Board was sceptical. No budget, no senior, no roadmap. I built the team, won gold on debut, made the institutional case, got the club formed — then spent a year making it undeniable. Open tournaments, women's inclusion, professional infrastructure, a brand built solo in Figma, and competitive results at regional level.

Polaris wants someone who can own cross-functional, high-stakes initiatives without needing a senior to hold the thread. That's a description of what I've already been doing — not a role I'm aspiring toward. I'm ready to do it at scale.
